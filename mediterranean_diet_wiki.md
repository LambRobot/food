# The Mediterranean Diet — Reference Wiki

*Compiled 2026-07-06 from authoritative sources (Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health, Oldways Preservation Trust, the Trichopoulou Mediterranean Diet Score literature, American Heart Association, Cleveland Clinic). Intended as the reference basis for a recipe scoring system.*

---

## 1. What the Mediterranean Diet Is

The Mediterranean diet is not a strict prescription but an **eating pattern** modeled on the traditional foods of countries bordering the Mediterranean Sea (Greece, southern Italy, Spain, etc.). Its defining characteristics:

- **Plant-forward:** the bulk of every meal is vegetables, fruits, whole grains, legumes, nuts, and seeds.
- **Olive oil is the principal fat**, replacing butter, margarine, and other solid/animal fats.
- **Fish and seafood** are the preferred animal protein.
- **Poultry, eggs, and dairy** (especially fermented — yogurt, traditional cheese) in moderate amounts.
- **Red meat and sweets are occasional**, not staples.
- **Minimally processed** foods; ultra-processed foods, refined grains, and added sugar are minimized.
- Wine in moderation with meals (optional/cultural), water as the main beverage.
- Emphasis on the *combination* of foods rather than any single "superfood," plus lifestyle factors (physical activity, shared meals).

> Key insight from Harvard: *"It is the combination of these foods that appears protective against disease, rather than individual components."*

## 2. The Mediterranean Diet Pyramid (Oldways, 1993)

Developed by Oldways, the Harvard School of Public Health, and the WHO. Organized by **frequency of consumption**, not portion size.

### Tier 1 — Base: Eat every day / most meals
The foundation of nearly every meal.

| Food group | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Vegetables** | The larger the variety and amount, the better. |
| **Fruits** | Fresh fruit as the typical dessert. |
| **Whole grains** | Whole wheat, oats, brown rice, barley, bulgur, farro, whole-grain bread/pasta. |
| **Legumes / beans** | Lentils, chickpeas, white/kidney beans, fava, peas. Daily intake encouraged. |
| **Nuts & seeds** | Walnuts, almonds, pistachios, etc. (walnuts high in omega-3). |
| **Olive oil** | Primary added fat, preferably extra-virgin. |
| **Herbs & spices** | Used in place of salt for flavor. |
| **Water** | Main beverage. |

### Tier 2 — Weekly: Eat often, in moderate portions
| Food group | Frequency |
|---|---|
| **Fish & seafood** | **At least twice a week** — the preferred animal protein. Oily fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel, anchovies) especially valued for omega-3. |
| **Poultry** | A few times a week, moderate portions. |
| **Eggs** | Daily to weekly, moderate. |
| **Dairy** | Especially fermented — yogurt and traditional cheese — frequently but in moderate portions. |

### Tier 3 — Top: Eat less often (a few times a month, or in small amounts)
| Food group | Guidance |
|---|---|
| **Red meat** | Beef, pork, lamb — a few times a month; use as a "condiment," not the centerpiece. |
| **Sweets & pastries** | Cakes, cookies, ice cream — special occasions. Fresh fruit is the everyday dessert. |
| **Processed/cured meats** | Bacon, ham, sausage, salami, hot dogs — the most strongly discouraged category. |

### Beverages
- **Water:** primary.
- **Wine:** optional, with meals — about 1 glass/day for women, 1–2 for men. Not recommended to start if you don't already drink.

## 3. Foods to Emphasize (the "good" list)

**Highest value (eat freely / daily):**
- All non-starchy vegetables (leafy greens, tomatoes, eggplant, zucchini, peppers, onions, garlic, broccoli, cauliflower, artichokes, fennel, cucumber, etc.)
- Fruits (berries, citrus, apples, pears, grapes, figs, melon, stone fruit)
- Legumes: lentils, chickpeas, beans, fava, peas
- Whole grains: bulgur, farro, barley, oats, brown/wild rice, quinoa, whole-wheat bread/pasta, couscous (whole-wheat)
- Extra-virgin olive oil
- Nuts & seeds (unsalted): walnuts, almonds, pistachios, pine nuts, sesame/tahini
- Herbs & spices; garlic, onion
- Fish & seafood, especially oily fish (omega-3)

**Good, moderate (weekly):**
- Poultry (chicken, turkey)
- Eggs
- Yogurt (plain), traditional cheeses (feta, parmesan, ricotta) in modest amounts
- Olives, avocado

## 4. Foods to Limit or Avoid (the "bad" list) — with severity

Ordered from *most* to *least* discouraged. This ordering drives the scoring penalties.

### Severity: SEVERE (strongly discouraged — "really bad")
- **Processed / cured / smoked meats:** bacon, ham, prosciutto, pancetta, sausage, salami, chorizo, pepperoni, hot dogs, andouille, lardons, cured meats. *The closest thing to a "not allowed" food.*
- **Sugary drinks / sodas** and large amounts of **added sugar** (as a primary component).
- **Deep-fried foods** / foods fried in non-olive fats; heavy use of hydrogenated fats, margarine, shortening, lard, tallow.

### Severity: HIGH (limit — a few times a month at most)
- **Red meat:** beef, pork, lamb, veal (especially fatty cuts). Occasional and in small portions.
- **Butter, cream, heavy cream, sour cream** and other solid animal fats used generously.
- **Refined sweets & desserts:** cakes, cookies, pastries, candy, ice cream, sweetened condensed milk.
- **Tropical/solid vegetable fats:** palm oil, coconut oil (high saturated fat), vegetable shortening.

### Severity: MODERATE (not great, use sparingly / don't exceed)
- **Refined grains:** white flour, white bread, white rice, white pasta, regular (non-whole) couscous.
- **Full-fat / high-saturated cheese used in large quantity** (e.g., melted cheese-heavy dishes).
- **Added sugar in modest amounts** (honey/sugar as a flavoring is more tolerable than as a base).
- **High-sodium ingredients:** soy sauce, fish sauce, bouillon, large amounts of added salt (flavor primarily from herbs/spices is preferred).
- **Mayonnaise and creamy dressings.**

### Severity: MILD (minor demerit / context-dependent)
- **Non-EVOO neutral oils** (canola, vegetable, sunflower) used in place of olive oil.
- **Starchy preparation methods** (large amounts of white potato as the main starch).
- **Moderate alcohol beyond wine** (spirits, liqueurs).

## 5. The Trichopoulou Mediterranean Diet Score (MDS) — the scientific gold standard

The most widely used research instrument (Trichopoulou et al., 1995; revised 2003, *NEJM*). It scores adherence on a **0–9 scale** using nine components. It's population-relative (uses median intakes), but the *direction* of each component is what we adapt for recipe scoring:

**Beneficial components (higher intake = better, +1 each):**
1. Vegetables
2. Legumes
3. Fruits & nuts
4. Cereals (whole grains)
5. Fish
6. Ratio of monounsaturated → saturated fat (MUFA:SFA) — i.e., olive oil vs. animal/solid fat

**Detrimental components (lower intake = better, +1 each):**
7. Meat & poultry (red/processed meat especially)
8. Dairy (particularly full-fat)

**Moderation component (+1):**
9. Moderate alcohol (wine) intake

> The direction of these components — reward vegetables/legumes/fruit/nuts/whole grains/fish/olive oil; penalize red & processed meat and heavy full-fat dairy; reward a high olive-oil-to-saturated-fat ratio — is the conceptual backbone of the recipe scoring system in `mediterranean_scoring_system.md`.

## 6. Practical Rules of Thumb (used as scoring heuristics)

1. **Olive oil in, butter/cream/lard out.** A recipe using EVOO scores better than the same dish using butter or cream.
2. **Fish > poultry > red meat > processed meat.** Protein choice is a major swing factor.
3. **Whole grain > refined grain.** Brown rice/whole-wheat pasta beat white.
4. **More plants = higher score.** Vegetables, legumes, fruit, nuts, herbs raise the score.
5. **Legumes are a signature ingredient** — their presence is a strong positive marker.
6. **Cooking method matters:** roasting, grilling, steaming, braising, sautéing in olive oil are good; deep-frying (especially in non-olive fat) is penalized.
7. **Sweets and heavy desserts** are inherently off-pattern; judged leniently only as occasional treats.
8. **Herbs & spices** replacing salt are a small positive; heavy sodium sources are a small negative.
9. **Ultra-processed convenience ingredients** (canned condensed soups, processed cheese products, packaged sauces) drag a recipe down.
